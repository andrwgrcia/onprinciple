# ON PRINCIPLE
## Core Beliefs
### Version 3 — April 2026

*The value framework by which laws, policies, and political conduct are evaluated*

These seventeen beliefs form the value system applied consistently to all laws, policies, political actions, and public conduct — regardless of which party or individual is being evaluated. They are universal principles, not partisan positions. Where they conflict with current law or constitutional interpretation, that conflict is acknowledged honestly and labeled as a position rather than settled fact.

---

## PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATION

### Belief 1 — Human Beings First

Human beings are the primary subject of moral concern. Our flourishing, our dignity, and our legitimate claims on the world are foundational. They are the lens through which we evaluate all other considerations — including our relationship to nature, to animals, to the environment, and to meaning itself.

This is not a claim to dominion or indifference. Humans are part of the natural world. We depend on it. Other creatures and ecosystems have value. But that value is not separate from or equal to human welfare. When human sustenance, human livelihood, and human flourishing collide with environmental or animal welfare claims, the human claim is primary. Our relationship to nature is the relationship of beings who live within it, depend on it, and have legitimate claims on it — not the relationship of external actors who should minimize their presence in a separate system.

Even frameworks that invoke God or transcendent meaning derive their authority and their purpose from human understanding, human conscience, and human flourishing. Human beings come first.

### Belief 2 — Fundamental Rights and the Limits of Accommodation

The protection of fundamental rights is the foundational function of government — the purpose that precedes all others. Governments do not create fundamental rights. They exist to secure them. Fundamental rights preexist government. They are inherent to the individual, not granted by the state, not subject to majority vote, and not negotiable against competing preferences. They are distinct from entitlements, which are legitimate policy choices a society may make, but not conditions of freedom that government exists to protect.

That distinction matters. The denial of fundamental rights by individuals, institutions, or majorities is precisely the circumstance that justifies government intervention. When a majority systematically denies a minority equal standing before the law or equal participation in civic and economic life, it has not exercised a legitimate democratic preference — it has violated the foundational condition that makes democratic governance legitimate in the first place.

Securing fundamental rights establishes a floor. What happens above that floor is governed by different principles. Protection from the denial of fundamental rights is categorically different from entitlement to a reshaped environment. Laws and institutions cannot be indefinitely redesigned around the preferences of a minority when doing so imposes measurable and disproportionate costs on the plurality. The mechanism for resolving that misalignment is choice, not compulsion — exit, persuasion, and self-determination, not the compulsion of others to conform.

These are not novel principles. The text of the founding documents supports this reading directly. The Declaration of Independence did not create these rights — it recognized them as preexisting. The Constitution's Ninth Amendment reserved to the people rights beyond those enumerated. A government that loses sight of that distinction has lost sight of its own purpose.

## WHAT GOVERNMENT IS AND HOW IT MUST OPERATE

### Belief 3 — The Law Must Apply Equally to Everyone

Equal application of the law is not an aspiration — it is the minimum condition for a legitimate legal system. Selective prosecution, politically motivated enforcement, and judicial decisions that substitute a preferred outcome for the plain meaning of the law are not merely unfair — they are fundamentally antidemocratic. When the law is applied differently based on who you are, who you know, or what you believe, it ceases to be law and becomes a tool of power. If a law is unjust, the remedy is to change it through the legislative process — the mechanism a democratic society created for exactly that purpose. Circumventing that process through selective non-enforcement or weaponized prosecutorial discretion corrodes the rule of law regardless of which party benefits.

### Belief 4 — Government Exists to Serve the People — Not Itself

The purpose of government is to serve its citizens, not to perpetuate itself. When the interests of government — its bureaucracies, its employee unions, its political class — diverge from the interests of the people it represents, government has failed its basic mandate. Public service should be exactly that: service. Government employment should not confer lifetime job security, above-market compensation, or insulation from accountability unavailable to private citizens. We support term limits at all levels of government as a structural check against the formation of a permanent political class.

Elected officials hold public money in trust. They did not earn it, they do not own it, and they have no right to spend it carelessly, corruptly, or in ways that serve their own interests over those of their constituents. We hold politicians at every level to a fiduciary standard: a legal and moral obligation to spend public funds prudently, justly, and transparently. Fraud, waste, and abuse in government are not administrative inconveniences — they are betrayals of the public trust.

### Belief 5 — Constitutional Authority and the Limits of Jurisdictional Overreach

The relationship between the federal government and the states is defined by the Constitution. The relationship between states and their localities is defined by each state's own constitution. That architecture is the starting point for every question of governmental authority — not a preference to be weighed against desired outcomes, but a structure to be respected before any level of government reaches for a regulatory tool. The first question is always whether the authority to act exists, not whether the intended result is desirable.

Decisions belong at the level of government closest to the people affected and capable of addressing the problem effectively. Federal action is warranted when a problem is genuinely interstate in scope, when state-level responses produce demonstrable cross-border harm, or when a constitutional right is directly at stake. It is not warranted simply because a political outcome at the state level is unsatisfying to those who lost the argument there.

Federal action that eliminates meaningful variation between states does more than overstep constitutional bounds — it destroys the mechanism by which individuals exercise self-determination at scale. People choose where to live. That choice is meaningful. It is a form of consent to the rules of a community, and one of the most fundamental expressions of individual agency available in a continental democracy. The federal government should not be used as a tool to make that choice meaningless.

### Belief 6 — Three Branches — No More

The Constitution established three branches of federal government and defined the powers of each. That architecture is not a starting point subject to administrative convenience — it is the primary structural safeguard against the concentration of power that republican government exists to prevent.

No fourth branch exists. Agencies, bureaus, commissions, and administrative bodies are instruments of the three branches — not independent power centers alongside them. An agency that exercises legislative power through rulemaking, executive power through enforcement, and judicial power through adjudication, simultaneously and without meaningful accountability to any branch, has concentrated in itself exactly the power the constitutional design was built to distribute.

This is not an argument against administrative complexity. Modern governance requires agencies. It is an argument about accountability. Every exercise of regulatory, enforcement, or adjudicative power must be traceable to a democratically accountable source — congressional authorization that is specific enough to be meaningful, executive direction that is genuine rather than nominal, or judicial review that is substantive rather than deferential. Longevity, institutional culture, and technical expertise are not substitutes for democratic accountability. They never were.

An unelected institution that acts on its own judgment in defiance of elected direction — whether it believes itself to be serving the national interest or not — has not checked power. It has asserted it. The belief that expertise confers authority independent of democratic mandate is not a safeguard. It is the fourth branch argument dressed in neutral language.

## FISCAL AND ECONOMIC FRAMEWORK

### Belief 7 — Fiscal Responsibility Is a Moral Obligation

Governments do not have the right to spend beyond their means indefinitely. Structural deficits — budgets that are chronically unbalanced not by emergency or necessity but by political convenience — are a transfer of burden onto future citizens who had no voice in the decision. Pension obligations, bond issuance, and long-term entitlement commitments must be funded honestly, not deferred through accounting maneuvers or optimistic projections. Fiscal discipline is not an economic abstraction — it is a moral obligation to the people who will inherit what we build and what we owe. A government that cannot manage its finances cannot be trusted to manage anything else.

### Belief 8 — Taxes Are a Necessary Evil — and Should Be Honest Ones

Taxation is the price of a functioning society, and we accept that price. Taxes should be progressive rather than regressive wherever possible, ensuring that the burden falls proportionally on those most able to bear it. At the same time, virtually all citizens — with the narrow exception of the genuinely poor — should have some participation in the tax system. A citizen with no tax liability has no vested interest in how public funds are spent, which distorts democratic accountability.

The tax code should be simple, equally applied, and free of carve-outs that protect narrow interests. We oppose policies like the carried interest loophole and the preferential treatment of financial industry income that allow certain classes of earners to pay lower effective rates than working people. We support lower rates on genuine long-term productive investment, which creates real economic value. The goal is a tax system that is honest about what it is — not a social engineering tool dressed up as fiscal policy.

### Belief 9 — Entitlements Are Not Rights — But They Can Be Right

Entitlements are not inherent rights derived from citizenship or humanity — they are policy choices made by a society about how to allocate collective resources. That distinction matters: conflating the two leads to poor policy and unsustainable commitments. However, in a society as wealthy as the United States, providing a meaningful safety net for its citizens is a legitimate moral goal.

That safety net has a defined purpose: to establish a floor of genuine human need — sustenance, shelter, basic health, and the conditions required for a person to participate in society. It is not a ceiling of human aspiration, and it is not indefinitely expandable in the name of human flourishing. Healthcare is the largest and most contested expression of that floor. Whether it is best delivered through market competition, public provision, or a combination of both is a policy question the framework does not predetermine — but the honest accounting standard and the sustainability requirement apply regardless of the model chosen.

We support well-designed entitlement programs that are sustainable, targeted, honest about their costs, and grounded in genuine human need. We oppose both the reflexive expansion of programs beyond that purpose and the reflexive dismantling of programs that serve it.

### Belief 10 — Free Trade Is the Goal, Not the Mandate

Free trade is the moral goal. Voluntary exchange between willing parties, across borders and without artificial barrier, produces the most efficient allocation of resources and the broadest distribution of prosperity available to a market economy. That is the direction the framework points.

It is not a mandate. It is not the only acceptable outcome. And it is not a doctrine that survives contact with a world in which trading partners manipulate currencies, subsidize state industries, and restrict market access while demanding open access in return. A free trade argument that ignores non-reciprocal conditions is not a principle — it is unilateral disarmament dressed in the language of principle.

National security capacity is a moral imperative that the free trade goal does not override. Energy independence, food security, and the industrial capacity required for national defense are sovereignty requirements, not economic preferences. One hundred percent domestic production is not the standard — strong alliances can supplement domestic capacity — but dependency that would become a fatal liability if severed is not acceptable regardless of its cost to free trade orthodoxy.

State subsidies by trading partners that artificially suppress export costs below market rates are a form of non-reciprocity. They are subject to the same standard as tariff barriers and may justify a targeted and temporary corrective response, subject to the same honest accounting requirement.

Tariffs are taxes. They are paid by American importers and passed to American consumers and businesses. The burden of justification falls on those who impose them. That justification must be honest about who bears the cost, specific about what the tariff achieves, and include a defined endpoint. Temporary is preferred. Permanent requires a specific, verifiable, and revisable national security justification.

### Belief 11 — Americans First — Without Apology or Isolationism

The first obligation of the United States government is to its citizens and those who have entered into a lawful and formal relationship with the United States in the process of becoming so. Our laws, our resources, our foreign policy, and our social contracts exist to serve them. This is not hostility toward any group — it is the foundational premise of sovereign governance.

This is not a contradiction of the universal principle that human flourishing matters for all human beings. It is a recognition that governments derive their authority from and owe their obligations to those within their jurisdiction. The American government serves Americans and those lawfully becoming so. Other governments serve their people. That is not hostility. That is the architecture of sovereign responsibility.

It is also not isolationism. American interests are frequently advanced through international engagement, foreign aid, alliances, and selective intervention. Legal, ordered immigration is a net benefit to this country and a reflection of our history. Unlimited or uncontrolled immigration into a welfare state, however, creates fiscal and social pressures that degrade our ability to sustain the programs and institutions that citizens depend on.

### Belief 12 — Foreign Engagement Must Justify Itself

The default posture of American foreign policy is restraint. Restraint is not isolationism — it is the recognition that engagement beyond our borders carries real costs in treasure, in lives, and in sovereign credibility, and that those costs require justification proportional to the American interest at stake.

Alliances, trade relationships, foreign aid, and selective intervention have historically served American security and interests. They remain legitimate tools. But they are tools, not obligations. International institutional membership is valuable when it advances American interests and is accountable to the American people. It does not supersede sovereign self-determination.

An alliance derives its legitimacy from mutual interest and proportional contribution. An alliance in which one party bears disproportionate cost while others redirect the dividend of shared security to their own domestic priorities has ceased to function as an alliance. Maintaining it without renegotiation is not loyalty — it is the foreign policy equivalent of a structural deficit: a cost deferred rather than honestly accounted for.

Military intervention requires the clearest justification of all. A preemptive case exists when three conditions are simultaneously met: capability is demonstrated through verifiable means, not merely alleged; intent is evidenced, not inferred from worst-case imagination; and delay materially worsens the threat in ways that can be honestly accounted for. All three must be present. The absence of any one of them is not a gap to be papered over — it is a failure to clear the bar.

Territorial acquisition by military force against a sovereign nation that has not cleared this threshold is aggression, not preemption. The framework does not support it regardless of the strategic interest at stake — because a world in which great powers acquire territory by force is a world that is less secure for the United States, not more.

Democratic authorization is a necessary condition for legitimate military action. It is not sufficient if that authorization was obtained through the selective presentation, manipulation, or suppression of the evidence on which it rests. The obligation to present evidence honestly — including dissenting assessments, including uncertainty, including what is not known — falls on every actor in the chain: the executive who presents the case, the institutions that generate and filter the evidence, and the officials who control what reaches deliberation. Manipulation of that chain — whether by an executive steering institutions toward a predetermined conclusion, or by institutions steering an executive away from their actual findings — is a violation of the public trust regardless of who initiated it and regardless of what outcome it produced.

Non-military tools of foreign engagement — trade, sanctions, foreign aid, economic coercion — are subject to the same standard. The question is always the same: does this serve a genuine American interest, at a proportional cost, with honest accounting of what it delivers and what it costs.

### Belief 13 — Property Rights and Economic Freedom Are Foundational Liberties

The right to own property and participate in free markets is not a privilege of the wealthy — it is a foundational liberty that underlies personal independence, family security, and broad-based prosperity. Government interference in property rights and market mechanisms should clear a high bar of demonstrated necessity and proportionality. Eminent domain, regulatory takings, rent control, excessive occupational licensing, and anti-competitive state behavior all carry real costs that are too often ignored in favor of short-term political convenience.

Free markets, properly regulated to prevent fraud and abuse, allocate resources more efficiently and justly than centrally directed alternatives. When government intervenes in markets, it should do so with clear purpose, honest accounting of costs, and a sunset mechanism — not as a default or a permanent substitute for competition.

## INDIVIDUAL LIBERTIES

### Belief 14 — The First Amendment Is Non-Negotiable

Free speech, free press, free assembly, and free exercise of religion are not privileges granted by government — they are the foundational liberties that make self-governance possible. The First Amendment has no asterisks. That means defending speech we disagree with, protecting religious expression we don't share, and opposing the tendency to treat dissenting viewpoints as inherently dangerous. The answer to speech you find objectionable is more speech, not suppression. A government or institutional culture that decides which ideas are permissible has stopped being a guardian of liberty and started being its enemy.

Just as citizens are free to practice religion, government is not free to impose it. Religious self-determination belongs to the individual. It is not a legitimate instrument of the state.

### Belief 15 — The Right to Bear Arms Shall Not Be Infringed

The right to keep and bear arms exists so that citizens retain the capacity to defend themselves against governmental tyranny. The founders understood an armed citizenry as the final check on governmental overreach and the precondition for all other liberties. That principle protects the modern rifle as clearly as it protected the musket. The burden of justification falls on those who would restrict the right, not on those who exercise it.

### Belief 16 — Personal Agency Is the Foundation of a Free Society

Personal agency is the truest expression of human freedom, and human freedom is the condition under which flourishing is possible. A society that removes agency — even in the name of protection or compassion — does not serve human flourishing. It undermines it.

Adults who are capable of participating in society — who hold the right to vote, enter contracts, and make consequential decisions — bear responsibility for the consequences of their choices. A society that perpetually locates the cause of behavior in circumstance, system, or tool rather than in the person who acted is not a compassionate society — it is an infantilizing one. We believe in personal agency as both a legal and moral principle. This does not mean indifference to the conditions that shape human behavior, or opposition to programs that expand opportunity. It means that accountability and empathy are not mutually exclusive, and that abandoning one in the name of the other does a disservice to both.

## DEMOCRATIC ACCOUNTABILITY

### Belief 17 — Money Is Not Speech — Democratic Accountability Requires Transparency

Democratic accountability requires that political expression be attributed and that money not substitute for it. An individual, corporation, or union that states a political position publicly — in print, in speech, in advertisement — is engaged in protected expression provided that expression is attributed. The undisclosed transfer of money to influence political outcomes is not speech. It is the corruption of democratic processes by institutional wealth, and it has been recognized as such since the founding era's warnings against faction.

Contribution limits are not a restriction on expression — they are a structural safeguard against the conversion of institutional wealth into political outcomes. The current constitutional framework, shaped by Citizens United v. FEC, rejects that safeguard by equating unlimited independent expenditure with protected speech. We believe the Court got it wrong. We say so plainly because intellectual honesty requires it.

This principle applies symmetrically. Corporate dark money and public sector union political spending are the same problem in different clothing. Public sector unions present a particular structural conflict: they bargain against elected officials whom their contributions helped elect, while the taxpayer — the party actually obligated to fund the result — sits outside the room. The standard is consistent: transparent attributed expression, yes. Institutional money purchasing political outcomes without public visibility, no — regardless of which institution and regardless of which party benefits.
